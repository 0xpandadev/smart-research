# Statistics And Data Lenses

Use when the research depends on public statistics, surveys, datasets, dashboards, charts, polls, market data, benchmarks, or quantitative claims.

## Data Quality Checklist

For each important statistic, inspect:

- producer, methodology, and incentive
- population, sample, sampling frame, geography, and time period
- definition of the metric and whether definitions changed over time
- unit, denominator, currency, inflation adjustment, seasonality, and revisions
- confidence interval, margin of error, or uncertainty if available
- missing data, survivorship bias, selection bias, and reporting lag

## Source Hierarchy

Prefer:

- official statistical agencies, regulators, central banks, public datasets
- peer-reviewed or methodologically transparent datasets
- company filings and audited disclosures for company-specific facts
- reputable aggregators only when primary data is unavailable or too fragmented

## Interpretation Rules

- Do not compare numbers with different denominators, geographies, or periods without warning.
- Do not infer causality from correlation.
- Keep raw reported values separate from normalized, adjusted, or estimated values.
- For charts, extract axes, labels, units, date range, and footnotes before interpreting the trend.

## Output Checks

- Include the exact metric name used by the source.
- Include period and unit.
- Flag methodology gaps rather than hiding them.
- If the data is too weak, say what would be needed to make the conclusion stronger.
